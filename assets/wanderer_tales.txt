A stranger passed through today, cloak dusted with road-grime from places nobody here has named.
They say the wanderer's eyes never settled on any one thing for long, like they were still walking even standing still.
The traveler spoke of a village on the far side of the hills where the wells run dry every third year.
Before leaving, the stranger left a single coin on the well's edge, old and marked with symbols nobody could read.
They asked strange questions about the village's oldest houses, then wouldn't say why.
The wanderer carried a walking stick carved with a spiral that seemed to shift when you weren't looking straight at it.
Someone swears the stranger's shadow fell the wrong way at midday, though nobody else noticed it happen.
The traveler warned, half-joking, half-not, to keep the fires burning through the next new moon.
They spoke a few words in no language anyone recognized, then laughed like it was an old joke.
The stranger refused all offered food except a single apple, which they ate slowly, watching the village the whole time.
Before the gates, the wanderer paused, turned back once, and said the village was "closer to somewhere else than it thinks."
They left behind a small bundle of dried herbs nobody has been brave enough to burn yet.
The traveler's boots left prints that faded unnaturally fast, like the ground didn't want to remember them.
Someone asked where they were headed next, and the stranger only smiled and pointed at the horizon in every direction at once.
The wanderer hummed a tune that three different villagers later swore they'd dreamed the night before.
They mentioned, almost in passing, a village that used to stand exactly where this one does now.
The stranger's pack seemed heavier leaving than arriving, though nobody saw them pick anything up.
Before vanishing down the road, the traveler thanked the village for "letting them borrow the quiet for a while."
They asked an odd question: whether anyone here still remembered their own name from before they were named.
The wanderer's parting words were simple — "you'll see me again, or someone who remembers being me."
